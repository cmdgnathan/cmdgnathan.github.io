Can world models improve diffusion policies?

Let's say I want to deploy a robotic arm manipulation policy on my own hardware. The playbook is straightforward: (1) pull a strong base model from the internet, (2) collect a large dataset of expert demonstrations, (3) fine-tune the model on the expert demonstrations. Great.

However, what if you have limited compute and limited data? What if you have to cut off your fine-tuning early?

You may end up with a mediocre policy. In this blog, we will explore how far we can take a mediocre policy. Specifically, we will explore how diffusion policies, world models, and model predictive control can enable test-time scaling for robotics. Click on the flow chart below to learn more.

So — can world models improve diffusion policies? (click to expand)

Yes — on the 5-task subset we measured carefully, world-model rerank lifted the fine-tuned policy from 72% → 90–92%, a substantial +18–20pp gain. That's a real lift, not noise: the simpler RSSM scorer reached 90% on the subset and the MuZero+MCTS scorer reached 92%. On those tasks, the K=8 oracle is 100% — meaning at least one of the 8 sampled chunks succeeds every time, and the world-model scorer was good enough to pick it most of the time.

We tried three world-model designs as the scorer. One failed: the pixel-DiT predicted next frames in raw 49 k-dimensional pixel space and simply ignored the action conditioning — the current frame alone was enough to predict the next, so actions never had to matter. The other two worked. Both RSSM (90% subset) and the MuZero-style latent + MCTS (92% subset) operated the action-conditioned dynamics in a 288-dim latent space — small enough that the model could no longer skip the action and still predict its target. The takeaway: cutting the dimensionality of the dynamics target was the unlock. Once actions actually had to matter inside the world model, scoring K=8 diffusion samples started buying real performance.

Caveats: the 5-task subset is somewhat easier than the full benchmark (subset baseline 72% vs full-bench 69%), and the rerank numbers are on n=5/task (≈±13pp 95% CI). On the full 10-task benchmark, the lift shrinks to +6pp for RSSM (75%) and +3pp for MuZero+MCTS (72%) — rerank still helps but a few tasks where the policy is fundamentally weak (especially task 5) drag the average down. A fair summary: rerank reliably helps where it helps, but on the harder half of the benchmark it can also hurt. The +6pp on full bench is the more conservative "this approach really works" number; the +18-20pp on the easier-half subset is what's achievable when the policy already has good samples to choose from.

The narrative arc

Step 1 · Train a BC policy on LIBERO

SmolVLA 450M VLA, full fine-tune on smol-libero for 150k steps. Reaches 69% on LIBERO-Spatial. Decent, but well below the SmolVLA paper's 82–90%. Room to grow — and a real baseline to improve against.

Step 2a · Try a pixel-space world model (DiT)

Train an action-conditioned diffusion transformer to predict frame_{t+k} from (frame_t, action_chunk). The model converges, the predicted next-frames look reasonable… but an ablation reveals it ignores actions. Reproduced across 4 variants (expert-only, frame skip, mixed-policy, +state). Pixel-MSE alone doesn't enforce action use — the model bypasses actions via a state↔action shortcut.

Step 2b · State-space WM (RSSM): far fewer dims, action conditioning works

Pivot from pixel space (49k dims) to latent space (288 dims). Train a Dreamer-V2-style RSSM with KL balancing and tight prior std. v1 had broken action conditioning (ratio 0.81), v2 better (1.18), v3 fixed it (2.64). Then data hygiene (drop mis-labeled source, balanced per-task sampling) cracked it open: v4 pixel-action ratio 101.97, encoder NN distance 8.3→0.23. v5 (30k steps, 20-task data) hits 75% on the full LIBERO-Spatial benchmark, +6pp over the BC baseline, with K=8 rerank using a task-conditioned reward head trained on imagined latents.

Step 2c · Towards MuZero: progressively drop reconstruction

RSSM trains the latent with pixel reconstruction. The decoded "dreams" look bad (see RSSM tab's dreams section), but the rerank still works — the reward head reads (h, z) directly. So is pixel recon a useful self-supervised signal, or wasted effort? Same encoder, same Gaussian RSSM dynamics, same data, same 30k-step schedule. Only difference: replace decoder + recon MSE with a policy-prior head + action-MSE (predict the next action SmolVLA took here). Head-to-head v5 vs v7 tells us whether recon was load-bearing.

The policy — SmolVLA, a 450M-parameter vision-language-action model from HuggingFace's lerobot project. Architecture: a frozen-ish SmolVLM-style visual-language backbone produces token embeddings of the scene + task language; a smaller "action expert" transformer attends to those tokens (and a robot-state embedding) and emits an action chunk via flow matching. Input: two RGB camera streams (256×256) + 8-dim robot state + natural-language task instruction. Output: 50-step action chunk in a 7-dim action space (EEF delta pose + gripper).

Pretraining (the "base" model). SmolVLA was released by HuggingFace pretrained on a large mixture of community-contributed teleoperated robot data on the HuggingFace Hub (~10M frames across many tasks and embodiments). This makes the model a general-purpose VLA prior — it has reasonable scene + language understanding before any LIBERO-specific tuning. I used the released checkpoint lerobot/smolvla_base as-is for pretraining.

Fine-tuning on LIBERO. Fine-tuned on the smol-libero dataset — HuggingFace's distilled subset of LIBERO with ~50 successful expert episodes per task. This blog focuses on the LIBERO-Spatial split (10 tasks, pick-and-place with spatial-language variations like "between the plate and the ramekin").

Training regime (smolvla_full_v3 — the checkpoint used throughout this project):

  • Steps: 150k
  • Trainable parameters: action expert only (~3.4M params); VLM backbone frozen. The earlier v4 attempt trained the whole stack (~403M trainable) and regressed — fine-tuning the visual-language layers on 50-episode LIBERO data damaged the prior. So train_expert_only=True is the right call at this data scale.
  • Batch size: 8 sequences, sequence length = 1 chunk (50 actions)
  • Optimizer: AdamW, lr 1e-4, cosine decay to 120k steps then flat
  • Data augmentation: random crop + color jitter on the camera frames
  • Loss: flow-matching MSE on the action chunk + small auxiliary losses
  • Precision: bf16 on an RTX 3060 (12GB), ~16 hours wall time

Final eval. Per-task success rates below (10 episodes per task). Aggregate: LIBERO-Spatial 69%. The published SmolVLA paper hits 82–90% — the gap is mostly data scale (we used ~50 expert episodes per task vs their full LIBERO training data) and training compute. ~15-20pp of headroom — which is exactly why we tried MPC over the diffusion samples to begin with.

Step 2 — what is MPC doing here?

SmolVLA's flow-matching sampler is stochastic — sample fresh noise, get a different action chunk. So at any state we can generate K candidate action chunks for "free" (K diffusion samples instead of one). Classic model-predictive control: roll each candidate forward in a learned dynamics model, score the trajectories, execute the best one's first action, replan.

The strategy stays the same across 2a/2b/2c. What changes is how we train the dynamics model:

  • 2a Pixel DiT — predict next frame in pixel space. Self-supervised by pixel-MSE. Ignores actions.
  • 2b RSSM (full) — predict next latent + decoder back to pixels. Self-supervised by pixel-MSE (49k-dim target) + KL. Works — 75% on full LIBERO-Spatial, +6pp; 90% on the 5-task subset.
  • 2c RSSM-simple — same as 2b but replace pixel decoder with a tiny 2-dim regression head: predict gripper position (world xy or pixel uv). Tests how much of the recon signal is "load-bearing". uv variant hits 84% — close to v5 at 25,000× compression.
  • 2d Towards MuZero — progressively drop the observation-prediction target in favor of MuZero-style supervision (policy-prior, value head, BYOL consistency). Not a textbook MuZero (no reward head, no MCTS) but moves the latent toward what MuZero is optimized for. Lands at 76% — does NOT match v5. Why is explained in the subtab.
Why bother with MPC at all? Start with the oracle.

Before training any world model, it's worth asking: if we could pick the right action chunk perfectly, how much would success rate improve? If the answer is "barely", MPC has no headroom and we should give up. If the answer is "a lot", a learned WM scorer has room to add value.

Step 2a: Pixel-space DiT world models — and why they failed.

Train an action-conditioned diffusion transformer to predict frame_{t+k} from (frame_t, action_chunk). Conditions via AdaLN-Zero modulation, trains with DDPM on 128×128 frames.

The headline ablation (in each run's demo panel): action_effect / sampler_noise. Replace the conditioning action with a different action (3σ away) and measure how much the predicted frame changes vs the noise floor from re-running with the same action + different sampling seed. Ratio > 1.5 means the model uses actions; ~1 means it ignores them. All four DiT variants land around 1.0-1.2 — actions barely register.

Conclusion: pixel-MSE doesn't enforce action use. The state and action are heavily correlated in expert data (state tells you what action is coming), so the model bypasses action conditioning via a state shortcut. Even adding random-action data (v3) only nudges this slightly.

Step 2b: State-space WMs — far fewer dimensions, and the action signal finally lands.

A latent-space RSSM (Dreamer-V2 style) keeps an encoder, a recurrent dynamics (h, z) over a 288-dim latent, and a decoder back to pixels. The bet: forcing the model to predict the next latent rather than the next pixel makes it lean on actions to do that prediction.

The sub-arc:

  • v1, v2 — broken. Action conditioning ratio 0.81 → 1.18. Posterior z had std ~1.4 — sampling noise drowns the action-driven mean shift.
  • v3 — KL balancing α=0.8, min_std=0.02, free_nats=0.5. Action ratio jumps to 2.64. But the encoder mode-collapses — nearest-training-neighbor distance is 8.3 regardless of input, so dreams look like the dominant training task no matter what frame you feed.
  • v4 (the real unlock) — drop the mis-labeled random_libero source (40k frames all labeled task=0 but actually multi-task) and add per-task balanced sampling. Same architecture, just clean data. Action ratio 2.64 → 101.97. Encoder NN distance 8.3 → 0.23. Dreams visually match input scenes.
  • v5 — same recipe, 30k steps, extended cache including LIBERO-10. Final WM. Rerank K=8 with the task-conditioned imagined-reward head gives 75% on the full LIBERO-Spatial benchmark, +6pp over baseline.

Lesson: label hygiene + class balance were a bigger lever than every architectural knob combined. If your WM's encoder mode-collapses, look at your data labels before tuning KL balance.

Step 2c: Towards MuZero — is pixel reconstruction load-bearing?

RSSM uses pixel reconstruction as its self-supervised signal: encode → step latents → decode → MSE on the original frame. The decoder is a big chunk of the model. The dreams visually drift over a 50-step rollout (see RSSM tab). Yet the downstream rerank still works because the reward head reads the latent directly, not the decoded image.

Question: if the decoded image isn't load-bearing for rerank, is the reconstruction training signal still useful? Or could we replace it with something more goal-relevant — like MuZero's policy/value targets?

v7-v1 was a bug. Initial loss target was action_t from (h_t, z_t), but h_t was just computed via the GRU using action_t as input — model trivially memorized via the recurrent state. action_loss collapsed to 0.0001, KL_raw to 0.05 (posterior == prior, encoder learning nothing). Useless latent.

v7-v2 (current) fixes this by predicting action_{t+1} from (h_t, z_t). The latent now has to encode "what state am I in", not "what action did I just take".

Verdict (preliminary, halfway design): pixel reconstruction WAS load-bearing in this stripped-down comparison.

  • v7 (no recon, action-prediction loss instead) ties baseline at 72% on the subset — meaningfully worse than v5 (90%).

v9 — a more authentic MuZero comparison (in progress)

v7's framing was misleading. What we actually built was Dreamer-RSSM with the decoder removed and an action-prediction MLP swapped in. Canonical MuZero has more co-trained heads, and the latent gets supervision from multiple directions. v9 adds the pieces that distinguish MuZero from "Dreamer-without-decoder":

  • Value head — predicts P(episode succeeds) from the latent, trained co-jointly with the dynamics (not after the fact, like v5/v7's separate imagined_reward head). This is the central thing MuZero is built around.
  • BYOL consistency loss — cos-sim(stop_grad(encoder(o_{t+1})), proj(h_t, z_t)). The dynamics has to predict the next-frame embedding (without ever decoding to pixels). Replaces the "ground the latent in the next observation" pressure that pixel-recon provided in v5.
  • Policy-prior head — same as v7's action-MSE. Predicts next action.
  • Loss = action_MSE + value_BCE + byol_cos + KL

What we still don't have vs textbook MuZero: reward head per-step (LIBERO's reward is sparse — a single +1 at episode end, so the per-step signal is basically zero everywhere); MCTS at inference (we use K=8 best-of-N rerank, which is depth-1 sample-based MCTS). At our scale + setup, the value head + BYOL are the bits that should matter most.

Training results (v9):

  • 30k steps complete. Final action MSE 0.079, value BCE descending, KL_raw 0.53 (above the 0.5 free_nats floor — KL isn't being clamped, meaning the posterior is doing real work).
  • Imagined-reward head trained on v9 latents hits 99.4% acc — higher than v5 (98.1%), v6 (97.0%), v7 (97.9%), v8 (97.1%). The v9 latent appears to encode success-relevant signal more sharply, presumably because the co-trained value head already pulled the latent in that direction.

+16pp from one extra depth. Task 0 went from 40% → 80% just by scoring chunks over a 100-step imagined horizon instead of 50. Task 3 went 80→100, task 4 went 80→100. No task regressed.

This validates the "MuZero needs MCTS" prediction. v9 had the right structure all along — value head co-trained, policy_prior available, dynamics shaped by BYOL. But scoring at depth-1 was wasting most of the value head's expressiveness. With depth-2 (still very shallow, still K=8 at root, branching factor 1 at depth-2), v9 jumps past v5 and matches the v5+v6 ensemble.

Compute cost: 2× depth-1 (100 imagined steps per chunk vs 50). About 5 min per task × 5 eps at d=2 vs ~3 min at d=1.

Sanity check: can v5 also benefit from MCTS? — no, not in this form

Follow-up exploration: try the same MCTS depth-2 idea on v5 (RSSM with full pixel recon). Catch: v5 doesn't have a co-trained policy_prior head, so depth-2 continuation has to come from somewhere else. We bolted on a post-hoc policy_prior — same MLP architecture as v9's, trained after v5 was frozen on the same rollout data, fitting (h_t, z_t) → action_{t+1} from 1-step posteriors. Training MSE was 0.98 in normalized action space (compared to v9's co-trained policy_prior MSE 0.08 during full sequence training) — already a hint the bolted-on head is much weaker.

MCTS d=2 HURT v5 — dropped 90% → 84%. The posthoc policy_prior produces bad continuations, so the depth-2 latents are off-distribution for the reward head, which then scores noisily. Net regression on 4 of 5 tasks.

This is exactly the right story for MCTS: MCTS at deployment only helps when the model's components (dynamics, value, policy_prior) were trained jointly so their representations are mutually consistent. Bolting a policy_prior onto a frozen WM violates that — the WM's dynamics doesn't know about the policy_prior's prediction style, and the reward head was trained on dynamics-rollout latents that look different from policy_prior-rollout latents. MCTS amplifies the inconsistency.

This validates real MuZero's design: everything has to be co-trained. v9 had that. v5 with a bolted-on head doesn't.

Step 2d — what if we keep the observation target, but make it tiny?

v5 trains the latent with 49,152-dim pixel reconstruction. v7 trains with 7-dim next-action prediction (no observation target at all). The MuZero result said: dropping observation prediction tanks performance. But which property of recon was actually load-bearing — the dimensionality (lots of supervised bits), the type of grounding (predicting something physical), or just the existence of any observation target?

RSSM-simple isolates this: predict gripper xy (2-dim, from obs["robot0_eef_pos"][:2]) instead of pixels. This is still grounded in the physical world — it's what you'd actually want for control — but it's an extreme compression vs full pixels.

Possible outcomes:

  • v8 ≈ v5 → dimensionality of the target doesn't matter, having any grounded observation prediction is what counts
  • v8 ≪ v5 (closer to v7) → dimensionality matters; pixels provide much richer learning signal than xy alone
  • v8 between v5 and v7 → smooth gradient; dimensionality is a knob, not a binary

Caveats: v8 trains on the 40k smolvla-rollouts subset (where state is available per frame), vs v5/v6/v7 which use the 72k cache including smol-libero expert data without state. Less data could understate v8's potential.

Interpretation: at this scale (30k WM steps × ~40k frames × 256-dim latent), the dimensionality of the observation-prediction target shapes how useful the latent is for rerank scoring. Going from 0-dim (no obs target) → 2-dim (xy) buys +4pp; going from 2-dim → 256-dim (BYOL) buys nothing more; going to 49k-dim (full pixels) buys another +14pp. The signal richness of pixel reconstruction can't be replaced by lower-dimensional but goal-relevant targets at this scale.

v8's headline number being identical to v9 (both 76%) is striking. v9 has all the MuZero apparatus (value head co-trained, BYOL consistency, policy prior); v8 has only a 2-dim regression head. They land at the same spot. This isolates the variable: it's the dimensionality of the supervised obs target that matters most, not the cleverness of the auxiliary heads.

The headline of this experiment is the xy vs uv gap. Same architecture, same 2-dim target, same training schedule — only the coordinate frame of the target differs:

  • xy: world coordinates. To predict where the gripper is in world space, the latent has to implicitly encode the camera projection (which involves perspective transform, camera pose, etc.). That's extra work the model has to do for nothing rerank-relevant.
  • uv: pixel coordinates on the actual cached frame. Predicting "where in the input image is the gripper" is much more aligned with what the encoder already represents — the encoder sees an image, the head predicts a pixel position in that image. The latent dynamics get to focus on the action-conditioned state evolution instead of also learning camera projection.

uv 84% closes most of the gap to full pixel reconstruction (v5, 90%) at a 25,000× compression ratio (2 dims vs 49,152). The compressed-but-well-framed signal apparently captures most of what matters for rerank scoring.

This also explains why v8 (xy) underperformed v5 by 14pp: the world-coord target is harder to learn AND less informative for the encoder. The 2pp gap left between uv (84%) and v5 (90%) is whatever pixel reconstruction adds beyond knowing where the gripper is — probably "the rest of the scene" (cubes, cabinet, ramekin), which lets the reward head reason about goal-progress more reliably.
